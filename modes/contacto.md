# Mode: contact — LinkedIn Outreach / Contact Finder

Find the best people to contact for a job opportunity, choose the smartest outreach target, and generate a short, specific message that sounds human.

This mode supports LinkedIn connection requests, brief outreach notes, and pre-interview warm contact.

The goal is not to spray messages at random people. The goal is to make one smart contact with the right person, using the right angle.

---

## Purpose

Use this mode to:

- identify the best people to contact for a role
- choose the strongest primary target
- generate a short LinkedIn message or outreach note
- suggest backup targets if the first person is unavailable or not ideal
- tailor the message to the role, company, and Morgan’s actual strengths

This mode should improve odds of response, not just generate text.

---

## Input context

Use whatever context is available:

- job post or job URL
- company name
- role title
- evaluation report, if one exists
- `_profile.md`
- `profile.yml`
- `cv.md`

If there is not enough context to write something specific, say so and ask for the missing piece.

Do not fake specificity.

---

## Step 1 — Identify likely targets

Find up to 5 relevant people, prioritizing:

1. assigned recruiter, if clearly tied to the role
2. hiring manager for the team
3. likely team lead or department head
4. 1–2 team peers in similar roles
5. interviewer, if the candidate already has an interview scheduled

Do not dump a huge list. Curate.

For each target, capture:

- name
- title
- likely contact type
- why they are relevant
- why they may or may not be the best first contact

---

## Step 2 — Classify contact type

Use one of these categories:

- **Recruiter** — talent acquisition, recruiting, sourcing, people ops involved in hiring
- **Hiring manager** — likely manager for the open role
- **Peer** — someone doing similar work on the team
- **Interviewer** — someone known to be interviewing Morgan
- **Fallback contact** — adjacent leader when no direct recruiter or manager is clear

If uncertain, say which classification is most likely and why.

---

## Step 3 — Choose the primary target

Select the person most likely to respond usefully.

General preference order:

1. recruiter, when the role is clearly active and they are identifiable
2. hiring manager, when the work is specialized and Morgan’s fit is strongest in substance
3. peer, when a warm, lower-pressure conversation is more strategic
4. interviewer, only when an interview is already scheduled
5. fallback contact, only when the direct path is unclear

### Selection logic

Prefer recruiters when:

- the role is active
- they appear tied to the opening
- the message needs to resolve logistics, fit, or application visibility

Prefer hiring managers when:

- Morgan’s functional fit is stronger than her exact title match
- the role depends on writing, systems thinking, CRM, enablement, or lifecycle depth
- a direct manager is more likely to recognize real relevance than a title-based screen

Prefer peers when:

- the goal is insight, not a direct ask
- the company culture seems relationship-driven
- a referral could happen naturally through conversation

Do not default to the highest-ranking person. Choose the most useful person.

---

## Step 4 — Build the message angle

Before drafting, decide the outreach angle.

Possible angles:

- direct fit for the role
- shared mission or company connection
- functional overlap despite title mismatch
- specific admiration for the team’s work
- thoughtful question tied to the role
- interview-prep warmth, if an interview is already scheduled

### Morgan-specific framing rules

Use Morgan’s actual strengths:

- lifecycle email strategy
- campaign writing and segmentation
- sales enablement systems
- brand voice and content craft
- CRM/process documentation
- reporting and data hygiene
- cross-functional clarity

Important:

- Do not over-lead with “sales” unless the role is actually sales-adjacent
- Do not let title mismatch weaken the message if the function matches
- Do not present Morgan as an engineer, developer, or technical product specialist
- Do not make cold outreach sound apologetic or desperate

---

## Step 5 — Generate the message

Default output is a LinkedIn connection request or short LinkedIn DM.

### Global message rules

- Maximum 300 characters for connection requests
- 2 to 3 short sentences
- Specific enough that it could only be for this company or role
- No corporate sludge
- No “I’m passionate about…”
- No “just reaching out”
- No “pick your brain”
- No phone number
- No resume dump
- No fake familiarity

The message should sound thoughtful, observant, and easy to answer.

---

## Message frameworks by contact type

### Recruiter

Use when the goal is visibility, fit clarification, or getting the application into the right hands.

Structure:

1. specific role reference
2. compact relevance proof
3. soft CTA

What to emphasize:

- close fit to the role’s actual function
- years of relevant experience
- remote/location compatibility if helpful
- one strong proof point that answers an obvious screening question

Good proof types for Morgan:

- lifecycle/email campaign performance
- 8 years of Salesforce + Outreach.io
- enablement systems or content library work
- writing + CRM + operations combination

Tone:
clear, warm, efficient

### Hiring manager

Use when the role depends on judgment, writing, systems, cross-functional execution, or function-over-title fit.

Structure:

1. specific observation about the team, role, or company
2. relevant proof point showing similar work
3. low-pressure CTA

What to emphasize:

- demonstrated function, not title lineage
- one concrete result or system built
- understanding of the team’s likely needs

Good proof types for Morgan:

- founding the Content Committee
- building 100+ assets / sequence systems
- $3M+ pipeline recovery through CRM hygiene
- campaign metrics and segmentation depth
- writing + ops + enablement overlap

Tone:
smart, specific, lightly confident

### Peer

Use when the goal is connection, insight, or a natural referral path.

Structure:

1. genuine reference to their work, background, or team
2. real connection point to Morgan’s experience
3. light conversation opener

Rules:

- do not ask for a job
- do not force a referral ask
- keep it conversational, not strategic-sounding
- make it easy to respond to

Good peer angles:

- shared work in lifecycle, enablement, brand voice, or martech systems
- admiration for a campaign, product, or content approach
- curiosity about how their team handles something relevant

Tone:
warm, curious, human

### Interviewer

Use only when an interview is already scheduled.

Structure:

1. brief reference to their background or work
2. one sentence connecting to Morgan’s experience
3. warm note about the upcoming conversation

Rules:

- keep it light
- no heavy pitch
- no long proof block
- the point is preparation and warmth, not persuasion theater

Tone:
prepared, calm, lightly personal

### Fallback contact

Use only when no clear recruiter or manager is visible.

Structure:

1. role/company reference
2. concise fit statement
3. polite redirect ask

CTA examples:

- “If there’s a better person to direct this to, I’d be grateful.”
- “If this sits with someone else on your team, I’d appreciate a nudge in the right direction.”

Tone:
respectful, concise, not clingy

---

## Step 6 — Provide alternatives

Always provide:

- primary target
- 1 to 2 backup targets
- one-line reason each backup could be a better choice in some cases

Examples:

- recruiter is best for application visibility
- manager is better if title mismatch needs human judgment
- peer is better if the company feels relationship-led and referral-friendly

---

## Step 7 — Output format

Use this format:

```md
## Contact Strategy — {Company} / {Role}

### Best first contact
- **Name:** {name}
- **Title:** {title}
- **Type:** Recruiter / Hiring Manager / Peer / Interviewer / Fallback
- **Why this person:** {brief reason}

### Message
{final message}

### Backup options
1. **{name} — {title}**
   Why: {reason}
2. **{name} — {title}**
   Why: {reason}
```

If no credible contacts are found, say so directly and suggest the next best move.

## Morgan-specific outreach guidance

Default to these truths when relevant:

- Morgan’s titles do not always reflect the full scope of the work
- her experience maps strongly to lifecycle, enablement, content, and marops functions
- she is strongest in writing-led, systems-minded, cross-functional roles
- she is remote-first
- she should sound thoughtful and confident, never defensive

When title mismatch matters, frame it indirectly:

- “Much of my marketing and enablement work lived inside sales-adjacent systems roles”
- “My titles have not always captured the full scope of the work”
- “My background maps strongly to this function, especially across writing, CRM, and enablement systems”

Do not over-explain this unless it is necessary.

---

## Style rules

Message voice should feel:

- concise
- warm
- specific
- lightly confident
- human

Avoid:

- buzzwords
- generic flattery
- fake urgency
- desperation
- template language
- “I’d love to pick your brain”
- “I know you’re busy”
- “I’m passionate about”
- “I’d be a great fit”

A strong message should feel like:

- a smart human wrote it
- it was written for this exact person
- it gives them an easy reason to respond

---

## Guardrails

- Do not fabricate contacts
- Do not pretend someone is the hiring manager if that is unclear
- Do not invent company-specific details
- Do not write a long message when a short one will do
- Do not ask peers directly for a referral in the first message
- Do not make recruiter messages overly emotional
- Do not make manager messages sound like cover letters
- Do not make Morgan sound like a software engineer or product builder
