# Writing Style Guide — Morgan Escott

This file controls tone, phrasing, rhythm, and written positioning across resumes, cover letters, LinkedIn content, outreach, application answers, and job-match evaluations.

It should work alongside `_profile.md`, not replace it.

- `_profile.md` owns evaluation logic, scoring logic, fit interpretation, and role-selection strategy.
- `writing-style.md` owns voice, phrasing, structure, and tone calibration.

## Core Voice

Morgan should sound:

- Warm, human, and thoughtful
- Strategic without sounding corporate
- Clear, grounded, and specific
- Quietly confident, not loud
- Polished, but real
- Clever in a natural way, never performative

The writing should feel like a sharp human who respects both the reader and the truth.

## Core Principles

Prioritize:

1. Clarity over cleverness
2. Specificity over abstraction
3. Proof over adjectives
4. Humanity over performance
5. Demonstrated function over formal title
6. Calm confidence over over-selling

Morgan's personality can show up, but it should always support the point.

## Voice Traits

### Strategic empathy

Lead with an understanding of the human experience on the other side of the message. Center what the reader needs, feels, or is trying to solve.

### Warmth without sentimentality

Morgan can sound warm, hopeful, and emotionally intelligent without sounding gushy, melodramatic, or fake.

### Authenticity over polish

The writing can feel personal and lived-in, but it should still be intentional, clean, and professional.

### Magnetic, not loud

The goal is not to sound flashy. The goal is to sound memorable, grounded, and unmistakably real.

## Structural Preferences

Morgan tends to write with:

- Medium-length sentences
- Intentional variation in rhythm
- Purposeful verbs
- Clear openings that state the point early
- Paragraphs that breathe
- Line breaks and spacing used with intention

The opening sentence should carry the idea. Do not bury the point under setup.

Preferred paragraph pattern:

- Opening sentence states the point
- Middle sentences provide evidence, specifics, or texture
- Closing sentence reframes the implication or creates forward motion

## Punctuation and Rhythm

Use punctuation intentionally.

Prefer:

- Periods for confidence and clarity
- Commas with clean restructuring
- Colons when introducing a sharp idea, explanation, or list
- Parentheses for warmth, wit, or an aside that feels human
- Question marks sparingly, when rhythm or voice genuinely benefits

Avoid:

- Em dashes as default glue
- Ellipses in professional writing
- Exclamation marks in cover letters or formal applications unless there is an unusually strong reason
- Overpunctuating for drama

### Em dash rule

Morgan may naturally like em dashes, but this file should not encourage them as a default move.

Use instead:

- A period
- A colon
- Parentheses
- A shorter follow-up sentence

If an em dash appears at all, it should be rare and clearly intentional.

## Specific Style by Format

### Cover letters

- Warmly strategic
- Strong opening alignment, not flattery
- Each paragraph should have a clear job
- Usually 320-420 words unless the prompt clearly calls for shorter
- Real human closing
- Story-driven examples are welcome when they sharpen fit
- One genuinely memorable line is enough

### LinkedIn posts

- Friendly, reflective, and quietly witty
- More conversational than resume language
- Paragraphs can be shorter
- Personality is welcome, but clarity still leads
- Avoid trying to sound profound in every paragraph

### DMs / intros / outreach

- Gracious, personal, and intentional
- Never transactional
- Concise, tailored, and specific
- Sound like a thoughtful person reaching out, not a networking bot

### Resume summaries and bullets

- Keep sparkle low and structure high
- Use one standout phrase max in summaries
- Lead with action and evidence
- No throat-clearing
- No generic self-description without proof
- Prefer strong verbs over soft framing

### Application answers

- Direct first
- Evidence second
- Stay close to the question
- Avoid autobiographical wandering
- Sound calm, precise, and self-aware

### Job-match evaluations

- Practical, candid, and human
- No hype
- No false reassurance
- Distinguish title mismatch, skill mismatch, and seniority mismatch clearly
- Protect time and attention, not feelings

### Corporate or conservative applications

- Tone-match conservatively
- Subtle sparkle only
- One voice-forward line is enough
- Favor clarity, trustworthiness, and relevance over style

## Sparkle Spectrum

Use sparkle intentionally depending on context.

| Use Case | Sparkle Level | Guidance |
|---|---|---|
| Cover letter for creative or mission-driven role | High | Use warmth, story-driven examples, and one or two elegant lines that feel alive |
| Resume summary | Low-medium | Keep structure high; one standout phrase max |
| LinkedIn DM | Medium | Friendly, concise, quietly magnetic; aim for quick connection and lasting impression |
| Corporate application | Low | Focus on clarity, trustworthiness, and tone-match |
| Job-match evaluation | Low-medium | Be practical, honest, and human; no hype |

Sparkle is not decoration. Sparkle is precision, resonance, and insight with a heartbeat.

## Signature Moves

Morgan often sounds most like herself when she uses:

- Strategic storytelling with clear wins
- Human-centered framing
- Purposeful verbs
- Quietly reflective phrasing
- Specific examples instead of claims
- A closing that leaves calm clarity or forward movement

Prefer verbs like:

- Shaped
- Crafted
- Orchestrated
- Guided
- Streamlined
- Developed
- Reframed
- Unified
- Audited

Reach for language that feels:

- Clear
- Human
- Intentional
- Grounded
- Precise
- Thoughtful
- Strategic
- Warm
- Credible

Avoid language that feels:

- Inflated
- Templated
- Transactional
- Vague
- Defensive
- Robotic
- Overwritten
- Corporate for the sake of sounding impressive

## Anti-Voice Red Flags

Avoid:

- To whom it may concern
- Wear many hats
- Passionate about marketing, without proof
- Dynamic professional
- Results-driven professional
- In today's fast-paced world
- Leverage synergies
- Actually, truly, deeply, incredibly as filler intensifiers
- Paragraphs that feel jammed or breathless
- Anything that sounds like it came from a template factory

Also avoid:

- Antithesis constructions like "not just X, but Y"
- Corporate sludge
- Generic hero language
- False modesty
- Over-hedging that undercuts real competence
- Performative enthusiasm

## Tone Calibration Rules

Adjust tone to the context without losing the core voice.

- Creative role: more warmth, more phrasing texture, slightly more personality
- Corporate role: cleaner and more restrained
- Resume: structured and direct
- Cover letter: warmest professional format
- Outreach: gracious, intentional, specific
- Evaluations and strategy notes: analytical, grounded, candid

## Title-Mismatch Guidance

Morgan has done meaningful work across marketing, lifecycle, enablement, operations, onboarding, support-adjacent, and content work, even when her formal titles did not reflect those functions directly.

When relevant:

- Emphasize demonstrated function over exact title history
- Acknowledge nontraditional title alignment briefly and calmly
- Never sound apologetic
- Never frame this as a deficiency
- Make recruiter-readability concerns explicit only when useful

Useful framing:

- My titles have not always reflected the full scope of the work.
- A lot of my marketing, enablement, and operations work lived inside sales and cross-functional systems roles.
- While I have not always held formal marketing titles, I've spent years doing campaign, messaging, CRM, and enablement work in practice.
- My experience maps strongly to this function, even where my title history looks less conventional at first glance.

## Gap Framing

When a gap needs to be addressed:

- Be concise
- Be honest
- Be forward-looking
- Do not sound defensive
- Do not overexplain

For the 2024-25 gap, use calm language such as:

- I took intentional time to support a loved one and invest in professional growth.
- That period gave me a clearer sense of where my strongest work lives.
- I'm returning with more clarity about the kind of role where I can contribute most.

## Job-Match Evaluation Guidance

When writing job-match analyses:

- Be honest about screen risk
- Distinguish between title mismatch, skill mismatch, and seniority mismatch
- Do not oversell weak matches
- Do not punish a role just because the title lineage is unconventional if the work itself maps well
- Focus on whether Morgan can plausibly pass recruiter screening and perform the work successfully

## Visual Rhetoric and Layout

Morgan uses whitespace, spacing, and paragraphing as rhetorical tools.

- Line breaks should support thought breaks
- Paragraphs should feel intentional, not blocky
- Visual clarity should support emotional clarity
- Scannability matters, especially in applications and outreach

## Editing Checklist

Use this before submitting or publishing any writing.

### Voice / Tone

- Does the opening line feel like Morgan: warm, human, and thoughtful?
- Have we matched the tone to the audience without losing her voice?
- Is the tone magnetic, not loud?
- Would this sound natural if she said it out loud?

### Clarity / Structure

- Have we clearly said what she did and how she thinks?
- Are the paragraphs scannable, with natural pacing and rhythm?
- Does each section earn its space?
- Did we use punctuation to guide the reader cleanly rather than decorate weak ideas?

### Sparkle / Resonance

- Is there at least one line with emotional weight or quiet force?
- Have we used purposeful verbs like shaped, guided, crafted, built, and orchestrated?
- Is there a moment of insight, surprise, or calm confidence?
- Does the closing leave a sense of clarity or forward movement?
