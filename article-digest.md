## Track 1: Email & Lifecycle Marketing

### The PTA Problem — and Why Those Numbers Are Hard to Get

The 74% open / 22% reply / 0 opt-outs PTA Council campaign is the one Morgan leads with for email roles — but the context matters more than the numbers.

PTA Councils are notoriously the hardest-to-reach audience in the K-12 yearbook space. They're volunteer-run parent groups with no professional email infrastructure. They change leadership every year, making list decay severe. They receive enormous volumes of fundraising solicitations, so their guard is up. And they often don't have a formal budget line for yearbooks, meaning the first email has to do three jobs: introduce the product, establish trust, and create urgency — all without triggering the "this is spam" filter.

Morgan didn't just write these sequences. She researched each school's PTA individually, found their Facebook groups, identified the current council leadership by name, used Thnks to send small personalized gifts (coffee, tree donation in their school's name) before outreach, and built a Vidyard video intro that addressed the specific school by name. The sequences used dynamic variables for school name, grade levels, and enrollment size so every email felt one-to-one.

Zero opt-outs across that audience is the number that operators notice. It means the writing earned trust rather than extracted it.

---

### The 85% Open Rate — and What "Hot Zone" Meant

Hot Zone (HZ) was Treering's internal designation for districts with the highest concentration of yearbook-buying schools in a geographic area. HZ outreach was competitive because multiple SDRs worked the same districts, and every SDR was writing their own sequences.

The HZ Spring 1st Touch (85% open / 39% reply, 89 contacts) was written by Morgan for the spring selling season — the second major campaign window after January. The prior year's sequences had been retired, and Morgan was rebuilding the first-touch approach from scratch. 89 contacts is a small cohort, but the reply rate at 39% means four out of ten people who opened it wrote back. That's not volume; that's precision.

### The January 2022 Suite — Scale + Consistency

The January 2022 suite is the campaign proof point that shows range, not just peaks. Six sequences. All above 50% open. Average 63% open across the full suite. 8.7% reply rate. 3,337 prospects added.

The January selling window was Treering's busiest outreach period — schools were returning from winter break, making decisions about the next year's yearbook vendor. Six sequences targeting different segments (district size, geographic region, prior-year engagement, contract status) had to be written, QA'd, and deployed simultaneously. All performed. None dragged the average down.

This is the evidence that Morgan's results aren't outlier flashes — they're repeatable across different audience types, different messages, and different volumes.

### Lifecycle Thinking: The "Yearbook Therapist" and Holiday Cards

Two sequence designs in the archive show Morgan applying lifecycle marketing logic before she had the vocabulary for it:

**The "Yearbook Therapist" templates (TR-0591):** Morgan built a set of UTM-tracked cold email templates across four school-type segments (general, Title 1, private, and HZ-adjacent). She self-titled the persona "Yearbook Therapist" — a positioning choice, not just a tagline. The sequence was a 4-touch structure with A/B variant testing baked in. UTM tracking on the links meant she could trace conversion activity back to specific touches.

**Holiday cards — 4 lifecycle stages (TR-0578):** Instead of sending a generic seasonal message, Morgan built four distinct holiday card versions keyed to where each school was in the sales cycle: active advisor, active chair, referral source, and went-cold. Each version had a different tone and CTA. The cards were signed "Morgan" — a deliberate brand choice to keep the communication personal and human, not corporate.

This is lifecycle segmentation. The audience wasn't separated by demographics — it was separated by relationship stage and treated accordingly. That's the same logic that underlies drip campaigns, onboarding sequences, and re-engagement flows in any modern lifecycle marketing platform.

---

### Personalization at Scale — the Handlebar Formula Library

Morgan maintained a library of Handlebars/dynamic variable formulas for Outreach.io sequences (TR-0717). These weren't simple first-name tokens. The library included district-level proof points, state-specific statistics, school-size-adjusted messaging, and custom snippet logic for variables like `{{snippet2}}` — which populated different sentences based on the prospect's district (e.g., "12,000+ nationwide, 2,000+ in [state], 47 in [district]").

The HZ Customer List (DF-0142) was essentially a formula generator: a lookup table that fed the right local proof point into the right email based on the district field in Outreach. Every email personalized to district scale was running off that infrastructure.

This is not "I use merge tags." It's a designed personalization system with a supporting data architecture.

---

### What "Cold Outbound" Means in Context

All Morgan's campaign metrics come from cold outbound sequences to school administrators who had no prior relationship with Treering. They were not warm leads, not existing customers, not re-engagement lists. They were contact-researched, freshly prospected targets.

For a hiring manager in email/lifecycle marketing: Morgan's results were achieved under harder conditions than most lifecycle marketers face. Warm audiences (existing customers, trial users, onboarded accounts) typically outperform cold outbound significantly. Her results are a floor, not a ceiling, for lifecycle marketing performance.

## Track 2: Sales Enablement

### The Content Committee — What It Actually Was

The Content Committee wasn't a committee in the bureaucratic sense. It was a cross-department content governance body that Morgan founded from scratch in January 2021 because no such structure existed.

Before: sequences were written independently by individual SDRs, with no shared voice, no QA process, no standard for what "good" looked like. Some sequences were excellent. Others went out under the company's name with typos, inconsistent tone, or messaging that contradicted the brand. There was no audit process.

Morgan proposed the committee, got buy-in from both the VP of Sales (Bobby) and the Marketing team, and built the structure: monthly meetings, a review cadence, a shared library of approved sequences, and voice/tone guidelines that became the internal benchmark. She chaired the committee and ran QA on every sequence before it went live.

The result: a library of 100+ email assets across all audience segments, all reviewed, all on-brand. When new reps joined, they had 129 Outreach sequences + 55 SDR Persona sequences available on day one. There was no equivalent to this at Treering before Morgan built it.

This is not a side project. It is a sales enablement program.

---

### The SDR Process Map — Still Live, Still in Use

The SDR Process Map (escottmorgan.wixsite.com/processmap) is an 8-step workflow website Morgan built to replace the ad-hoc, verbal-only new hire onboarding that existed before it.

Before the process map: onboarding happened through side conversations, tribal knowledge, and hope. There was no documented standard for how an SDR should work through a territory, when to escalate, or what tools to use at each stage.

Morgan designed the 8-step workflow, built the website (no IT support), and the site was formally adopted into the official new hire training deck. It's still linked and still used. When Morgan left in 2024, the infrastructure she built in 2021 was still the official onboarding resource for incoming SDRs.

Building a training asset that outlasts your tenure by three or more years is evidence of genuine enablement work, not just documentation.

### The Training Program — Live + Async

Monthly SDR workshops for 20+ employees across two formats:

**Live sessions:** Agenda-driven meetings with scenario practice, Q&A, and collaborative sequence critique. Morgan facilitated these — not as a manager running a performance review, but as a content authority running a craft session.

**Async materials:** Written documentation, annotated examples of sequences with notes on *why* a line worked, and recorded walkthroughs for new hires who joined after a session.

The reason both formats matter: the live sessions built culture around quality. The async materials gave reps something to return to six weeks later when they needed a refresher. A training program that only exists in live meetings is a presentation, not a system.

### The Kristina Negotiation — How the Content Committee Survived

The Content Committee didn't have an easy origin. In 2021, when Treering brought in Kristina as VP of Marketing, she moved to take over all sequence writing. This was a direct threat to the CC's existence and to Morgan's role within it.

The context: Kristina's sequences, like those of her predecessor Erika, didn't reflect Treering's brand voice. The team had specifically wanted to "get our voices back" — a phrase documented in a CC meeting record. Morgan had spent years building sequences that reps trusted. Kristina's sequences contained errors and didn't land the same way.

Morgan didn't just resist informally. She prepared a structured negotiation document (TR-0187) — "Kristina Bobby To Do, 1st Meeting" — for the meeting with Bobby and Kristina that would determine who owned what. The outcome was a formal split: Kristina would write the daily-driver sequences (broad, general-audience). Morgan would write the niche sequences: Title 1, Private School, HZ-adjacent.

The informal verdict on that split came from the reps themselves. Morgan's niche sequences were so preferred that SDRs started loading non-niche prospects into them and manually editing the verbiage to fit. This had to be stopped — it skewed the engagement analytics because non-niche prospects were contaminating the niche-sequence data. The problem created by Morgan's sequences being too good to leave for their intended audience is documented in archive notes.

This story matters for several reasons:
- It shows Morgan understood content ownership, stakeholder dynamics, and the politics of creative authority — not just writing ability.
- It demonstrates she advocated for quality in a situation where the institutional incentive was compliance.
- The outcome (niche-sequence dominance, reps preferring her work to the official daily-driver sequences) is the strongest possible validation of her brand voice judgment.

Morgan's journalism degree (KU, 2006–2008) and editor-in-chief role at KCKCC (2004–2006, 1.5 years) isn't listed for credential padding. It trained a specific cognitive habit: write for a reader who has no obligation to finish your sentence.
